Domain · Curiosity · Motivation

The gap between what technology promises cities and what cities can actually absorb

I remember being six years old, sitting in the backseat watching road construction out the window. Each trip was a chance to track what had changed, what was new, and what was taking shape. I knew those roads. But at six, everything still felt enormous and alive with possibility.

That early instinct is still alive within me: to pay attention to how pathways are built and how to navigate them, how people interact with the network, and what changes when the network changes.

Today I work at the intersection of urban infrastructure and digital governance. I study and navigate the systems that shape how cities evolve as advanced digital systems become the preferred infrastructure in urban areas — how we move, how we access opportunity, how we engage with one another, and who gets to participate in decisions about all of the above.

What drives me is a particular kind of curiosity: not just about what technology promises cities, but about what happens in the gap between the promise and the reality. That gap is where the most consequential questions about governance, equity, capacity, and trust live. It's also where I do my most useful work.

My Approach

Genuine inquiry before prescription

Complex problems don't usually announce themselves cleanly. They arrive wrapped in competing priorities, social and institutional histories, and unexamined assumptions. The first task isn't solving the problem. It's understanding what the problem actually is, and priorities of the stakeholders involved.

My approach begins there: with inquiry before prescription. That means interviewing the people closest to the problem, analysis of the documents and data that reveal how decisions have actually been made, and the kind of sustained contemplation that notices patterns across seemingly isolated domains. I move between quantitative and qualitative methods depending on what the terrain demands, not out of methodological habit, but because different questions require different instruments.

What I'm ultimately doing is synthesis that provides direction. I'm looking for the connections between things that appear unrelated, the tensions that explain why a straightforward solution hasn't worked, and the pathways that become visible only when you're willing to treat complexity as an opportunity.

In practice, this means I'm often most useful at the moments other people find most difficult — when the vision is clear but the path isn't, when stakeholders are misaligned, when a technically sound solution keeps failing for reasons nobody can quite articulate. Those are navigational problems. Navigation is what I do.

Publications

Research as applied intelligence

The work below isn't research for its own sake. Each piece addresses a specific gap in how decision-makers understand the challenges cities face as they navigate the digital transformation. The question driving all of it: what do people in positions of authority actually need to know to make better decisions?

The Transaction Costs of Municipal Cyber Risk Management

UC Berkeley Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity · 2024

When cities invest in cybersecurity, the sticker price is rarely the real cost. This white paper exposes the hidden costs that derail municipal cyber risk management: the time and resources consumed by procurement, contracting, coordination, insurance negotiation, and compliance that rarely appear in budget projections but consistently produce cost overruns and administrative strain. For technology companies selling into the public sector, this research is a map of where deals go sideways and why cities often can't absorb what vendors are offering, even when the product is sound.

Matching Technique with Authority: How Local DOTs Can Narrow the Gap Between Network Management Authority and Analytical Capacity

UC Berkeley Institute of Transportation Studies · Policy Brief & Research Report · 2025

Advanced traffic simulation tools can dramatically improve how cities manage mobility equitably — but only if the agencies responsible for traffic management have the capacity to use them. This research investigates the institutional gap between what the technology can do and what local departments of transportation are actually equipped to do with it. The practical implication: technology adoption in the public sector is as much a governance and capacity problem as it is a technical one. Companies that understand this build better partnerships and avoid costly failed deployments.

Smart City Governance and the Limits of Private Authority

Dissertation Research · UC Berkeley · 2025

When Alphabet's Sidewalk Labs attempted to develop a smart city testbed in Toronto, the project became a flashpoint for concerns about data governance and private sector overreach. Conventional analysis focused on public resistance. This research took a deeper look at why the project was structurally unable to succeed regardless of public sentiment. The finding: the Sidewalk Toronto partnership lacked genuine political authority from the outset, a deficiency produced in part by private sector attempts to accelerate business development by bypassing direct engagement with public agencies. For any company seeking to work with cities, this case study is a precise diagnosis of how that relationship can fail.

About

Rowland Awadagin Herbert-Faulkner, Ph.D.

I hold a Ph.D. in City and Regional Planning from UC Berkeley, with concentrations in transportation infrastructure, urban governance, and cybersecurity. My academic foundation began at MIT, where I studied Civil Engineering with a concentration in Urban Studies and Planning.

That combination — engineering and planning, infrastructure and governance, technical systems and the human institutions that manage them — has defined the arc of everything since.

Over the course of my doctoral work and beyond, I've been affiliated with UC Berkeley's Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity and the Institute of Transportation Studies. I've taught urban planning and transportation planning at the undergraduate and graduate levels, and I've presented research at venues the American Collegiate Schools of Planning annual conference and ITS California.

Before and alongside academia, I've worked inside the systems I study — as an analyst examining transportation economic impacts, as an intern with the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency, and as a researcher studying high-speed rail and regional transportation planning at MIT.

I am based in the San Francisco Bay Area.
