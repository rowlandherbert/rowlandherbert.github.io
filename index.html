I'm Dagin. [DA-jin]

I'm looking to join teams that work with tech companies and local governments to close the gap between what technology promises and what cities can manage.

Closing that gap involves taking a comprehensive account of the resource and relational requirements for:

  • The status quo
  • Implementing new systems
  • Maintaining the changes

That account should guide planning, deployment, integration, and maintenance. Without it, intended solutions quickly become expensive problems.

I'm interested in applying my background to plan and deliver purposeful tech solutions that actually work for cities.

I've published research on digital infrastructure governance, practiced transportation planning in the public and private sectors, and directed equity policy work with community organizations. Those experiences have focused my attention on what's necessary for successful technology adoption at the local level.

Applying research to practice

My work addresses specific gaps in how decision-makers understand the challenges cities face as they confront the digital transformation.

The Transaction Costs of Municipal Cyber Risk Management

UC Berkeley Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity · 2024

When cities invest in cybersecurity, the sticker price is rarely the real price. This white paper exposes the hidden costs that derail municipal cyber risk management — procurement, contracting, coordination, insurance negotiation, and compliance — that rarely appear in budget projections but consistently produce cost overruns and administrative strain. For technology companies selling into the public sector, this research is a map of where deals go sideways and why cities often can't absorb what vendors are offering, even when the product is sound.

Matching Technique with Authority: How Local DOTs Can Narrow the Gap Between Network Management Authority and Analytical Capacity

UC Berkeley Institute of Transportation Studies · Policy Brief & Research Report · 2025

Advanced traffic simulation tools can dramatically improve how cities manage mobility equitably, but only if the agencies responsible for traffic management have the capacity to use them. This research investigates the institutional gap between what the technology can do and what local departments of transportation are actually equipped to do with it. The practical implication: technology adoption in the public sector is as much a governance and capacity problem as it is a technical one.

Read the research →

Smart City Governance and the Limits of Private Authority

Dissertation Research · UC Berkeley · 2025

When Alphabet's Sidewalk Labs attempted to develop a smart city testbed in Toronto, the project became a flashpoint for concerns about data governance and private sector overreach. Conventional analysis focused on public resistance. This research took a deeper look at why the project was structurally unable to succeed regardless of public sentiment. The finding: the Sidewalk Toronto partnership lacked genuine political authority from the outset, produced in part by private sector attempts to accelerate business development by bypassing direct engagement with public agencies.
