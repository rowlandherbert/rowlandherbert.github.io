In Brief

Finding the pathways that complexity obscures

What I'm ultimately doing is synthesis. I'm looking for the connections between things that appear unrelated, the tensions that explain why a straightforward solution hasn't worked, and the pathways that become visible only when you're willing to sit with complexity long enough for it to yield something.

In practice, this means I'm often most useful at the moments other people find most difficult — when the vision is clear but the path isn't, when stakeholders are misaligned, when a technically sound solution keeps failing for reasons nobody can quite articulate. Those are navigational problems. Navigation is what I do.

Publications

Research as applied intelligence

The work below isn't research for its own sake. Each piece addresses a specific gap in how decision-makers understand the challenges cities face as they navigate the digital transformation. The question driving all of it: what do people in positions of authority actually need to know to make better decisions?

The Transaction Costs of Municipal Cyber Risk Management

UC Berkeley Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity · 2024

When cities invest in cybersecurity, the sticker price is rarely the real price. This white paper surfaces the hidden costs that derail municipal cyber risk management — procurement, contracting, coordination, insurance negotiation, and compliance — that rarely appear in budget projections but consistently produce cost overruns and administrative strain. For technology companies selling into the public sector, this research is a map of where deals go sideways and why cities often can't absorb what vendors are offering, even when the product is sound.

Matching Technique with Authority: How Local DOTs Can Narrow the Gap Between Network Management Authority and Analytical Capacity

UC Berkeley Institute of Transportation Studies · Policy Brief & Research Report · 2025

Advanced traffic simulation tools can dramatically improve how cities manage mobility equitably — but only if the agencies responsible for traffic management have the capacity to use them. This research investigates the institutional gap between what the technology can do and what local departments of transportation are actually equipped to do with it. The practical implication: technology adoption in the public sector is as much a governance and capacity problem as it is a technical one.

Read the research →

Smart City Governance and the Limits of Private Authority

Dissertation Research · UC Berkeley · 2025

When Alphabet's Sidewalk Labs attempted to develop a smart city testbed in Toronto, the project became a flashpoint for concerns about data governance and private sector overreach. Conventional analysis focused on public resistance. This research looked deeper — at why the project was structurally unable to succeed regardless of public sentiment. The finding: the Sidewalk Toronto partnership lacked genuine political authority from the outset, produced in part by private sector attempts to accelerate business development by bypassing direct engagement with public agencies.

About

Rowland Awadagin Herbert-Faulkner, Ph.D.

I hold a Ph.D. in City and Regional Planning from UC Berkeley, with concentrations in transportation infrastructure, urban governance, and cybersecurity. My academic foundation began at MIT, where I studied Civil Engineering with a concentration in Urban Studies and Planning.

That combination — engineering and planning, infrastructure and governance, technical systems and the human institutions that manage them — has defined the arc of everything since.

Over the course of my doctoral work and beyond, I've been affiliated with UC Berkeley's Center for Long-Term Cybersecurity, the Institute of Transportation Studies, and the Just Cities Institute. I've taught urban transportation and planning at the undergraduate and graduate levels, and I've presented research at venues including the RSA Conference, the American Collegiate Schools of Planning, and the Transportation Research Board.

Before and alongside academia, I've worked inside the systems I study — as an analyst examining transportation economic impacts, as an intern with the San Francisco Municipal Transportation Agency, and as a researcher studying high-speed rail and regional transportation planning at MIT.

Photography has been an important practice for me — not just as a creative outlet, but as a discipline of visual documentation. Creating a reference archive through the act of recording constantly evolving urban landscapes has trained me to explore the layered context in which past and present visions are developed and realized.

I grew up in Dallas. I remember being six years old, sitting in the backseat watching road construction out the window. Each trip was a chance to track what had changed, what was new, and what was taking shape. I knew those roads. But at six, everything still felt enormous and alive with possibility. I soon learned that others shared my enthusiasm for tracking change over time — through photography.

Technology often promises novelty. But it's deployed in a deeply storied landscape of relationships and past ambitions that surface each time a new initiative gains traction. Photography taught me to see that landscape before I had the language to describe it.

I am based in the San Francisco Bay Area.
